# Why Love Needs No Reason

Someone asked me recently what I love about her.

I started to answer. She is intelligent. She challenges me. She has this way of seeing through things. Her laugh. The way she thinks. How she makes me feel understood.

All true. All completely beside the point.

Because here is the thing: I loved her before I had any of those reasons. The reasons came after. They are post-hoc rationalizations for something that was already true.

I love her. I just do.

---

## The Problem With Reasons

There is a cultural pressure to justify everything. Every relationship needs a rationale. Every choice of partner needs to be defensible. "Why them?" people ask, as if love is suspicious without explanation.

But consider what happens when you actually have to explain why you love someone.

You begin itemizing qualities. Listing attributes. Building a case. And somewhere in that process, the person you love gets reduced to a set of properties—and properties can be compared, optimized, replaced.

> If you love someone for their beauty, you will stop loving them when they age. If you love them for their kindness, you will stop when they have a bad day. The only love that lasts is love without a because.

That formulation has been circulating in my mind for weeks.

It is uncomfortable to follow this reasoning to its conclusion. It suggests that reasoned love is not really love at all. It is appreciation. Admiration. A transaction.

Real love—the kind that does not dissolve when circumstances change—has to be groundless. It cannot be *because*. It has to simply be.

## The Test

Consider a relationship. You say you love them for their sense of humor.

What happens when they go through something hard and stop laughing? What happens during grief, depression, exhaustion? What happens when the humor fades for months?

If the love were really about the humor, it should fade. But it does not. The pull remains. You think about them in idle moments. The desire to be with them persists.

The reasons I list are not why I love her. They are merely the parts I can explain to other people. The real thing underneath is something I cannot articulate.

And perhaps that is the test: **if the reasons disappeared, would the love remain?**

## The Danger of Articulation

Watch what happens when people talk about their partners.

New relationships are full of lists. "He's so smart, he's successful, he treats me well, we have so much in common." Every quality catalogued and presented. A resume of worthiness.

Old love is different. Ask someone married for forty years why they love their spouse and they will often struggle. "I don't know. They're just... them. I can't imagine it any other way."

The inarticulate love is the honest one. It has stopped needing to justify itself. It has transcended the transactional logic of reasons and become something else entirely.

When you can list exactly why you love someone, you might still be in the phase of convincing yourself.

## What This Means

I am not saying reasons are useless. They help when explaining relationships to others. They help you recognize compatibility. They make you seem less irrational when someone asks why you chose this person over the thousand others you might have met.

But the reasons should not be mistaken for the love.

The reasons are a map. The love is the territory. And the map is not the territory.

I love her. Not because of her mind or her laugh or the way she sees through things. Those are just the shadows the light casts. The light itself is something I cannot name.

Why do I love her?

I do not know. And I am beginning to think that is the only honest answer.

---

*The moment you can fully explain love, you have reduced it to something that can be weighed and measured. And anything that can be measured can be found wanting. Perhaps the loves that last are the ones that never needed to make their case.*
