# Why Love Needs No Reason

Someone asked me the other day why I love jiu-jitsu.

I started to answer. It's physical chess. It's problem-solving with bodies. It teaches humility. It builds mental toughness. The community is great. It's efficient exercise.

All true. All completely beside the point.

Because here's the thing: I was doing jiu-jitsu before I had any of those reasons. The reasons came after. They're post-hoc rationalizations for something that was already true.

I love it. I just do.

---

## The Problem With Reasons

We're trained to justify everything. Every decision needs a business case. Every preference needs a rationale. "Why do you want that?" is the question we ask children, as if wanting is suspicious without explanation.

But think about what happens when you actually have to explain why you love something.

You start itemizing features. Listing benefits. Building a case. And somewhere in that process, the thing you love gets reduced to a set of properties - and properties can be compared, optimized, replaced.

> If you love someone for their beauty, you'll stop loving them when they age. If you love them for their kindness, you'll stop when they have a bad day. The only love that lasts is love without a because.

I didn't come up with that. Some philosopher did. But it's been bouncing around my head for weeks.

Real love - the kind that doesn't dissolve when circumstances change - has to be groundless. It can't be *because*. It has to just be.

## The Jiu-Jitsu Test

Back to jiu-jitsu. Let's say I love it because of the problem-solving aspect.

So what happens when I hit a plateau where nothing works? What happens when I get injured and can't train for months? What happens when the problem-solving gets frustrating instead of fun?

If my love was really about the problem-solving, it should fade. But it doesn't. I still want to go back. I still think about techniques at random moments. I still feel the pull.

The reasons I listed aren't why I love it. They're just the parts I can explain to other people. The real thing underneath is something I can't articulate.

And I think that's the test: **if your reasons disappeared, would the love remain?**

## This Applies to Everything

People. Places. Work. Art.

Whenever I find myself building a case for why I care about something, I get suspicious. Is this love or is this a pitch? Am I feeling something or am I selling myself a story?

My friend Jabid would probably push back here. He'd say something like: "But you have to be able to explain your values. Otherwise you're just operating on instinct."

Maybe. But I think there's a layer underneath values. A layer that values are built on top of. And that layer doesn't respond to reasons.

## Where I Land

I'm not saying reasons are useless. They help with communication. They help you find people who love the same things. They make you seem less crazy when someone asks why you drive 30 minutes to train three times a week.

But I'm wary of mistaking the reasons for the love.

The reasons are a map. The love is the territory. And the map is not the territory.

I love jiu-jitsu. I love late nights writing code when something finally clicks. I love conversations that go too deep too fast. I love history books that make the past feel alive.

Why?

I don't know. And I'm starting to think that's the right answer.
