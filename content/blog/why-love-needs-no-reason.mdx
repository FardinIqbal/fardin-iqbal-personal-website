# Why Love Needs No Reason

Someone asked Fardin Iqbal the other day why he loves jiu-jitsu.

He began to answer. It is physical chess. It is problem-solving with bodies. It teaches humility. It builds mental toughness. The community is excellent. It is efficient exercise.

All true. All completely beside the point.

Because here is the thing: he was doing jiu-jitsu before he had any of those reasons. The reasons came after. They are post-hoc rationalizations for something that was already true.

He loves it. He just does.

---

## The Problem With Reasons

There is a cultural training to justify everything. Every decision needs a business case. Every preference needs a rationale. "Why do you want that?" is the question posed to children, as if wanting is suspicious without explanation.

But consider what happens when one actually has to explain why one loves something.

One begins itemizing features. Listing benefits. Building a case. And somewhere in that process, the thing one loves gets reduced to a set of properties—and properties can be compared, optimized, replaced.

> If you love someone for their beauty, you will stop loving them when they age. If you love them for their kindness, you will stop when they have a bad day. The only love that lasts is love without a because.

A philosopher articulated that. The formulation has been circulating in Fardin's mind for weeks.

Real love—the kind that does not dissolve when circumstances change—has to be groundless. It cannot be *because*. It has to simply be.

## The Jiu-Jitsu Test

Return to jiu-jitsu. Suppose one loves it because of the problem-solving aspect.

What happens when one hits a plateau where nothing works? What happens during an injury that prevents training for months? What happens when the problem-solving becomes frustrating rather than engaging?

The reasons listed are not why Fardin loves it. They are merely the parts he can explain to other people. The real thing underneath is something he cannot articulate.

And perhaps that is the test: **if the reasons disappeared, would the love remain?**

## Universal Application

This principle extends beyond jiu-jitsu. People. Places. Work. Art.

Whenever one finds oneself building a case for why one cares about something, suspicion is warranted. Is this love or is this a pitch? Is there genuine feeling or merely a story being sold?

Jabid would likely push back here. He would say something like: "But you have to be able to explain your values. Otherwise you're just operating on instinct."

Perhaps. But there seems to be a layer underneath values. A layer that values are built upon. And that layer does not respond to reasons.

## Where This Lands

Reasons are not useless. They help with communication. They help one find people who love the same things. They make one seem less irrational when someone asks why one drives 30 minutes to train three times a week.

But the reasons should not be mistaken for the love.

The reasons are a map. The love is the territory. And the map is not the territory.

Fardin loves jiu-jitsu. He loves late nights writing code when something finally clicks. He loves conversations that go too deep too fast. He loves history books that make the past feel alive.

Why?

He does not know. And he is beginning to think that is the right answer.
